Access

One of the biggest challenges to backcountry skiing is access. The two major facits to this are private property and distance (and/or the complexity of the terrain). The connection between them is usually that to avoid private property, one must take the longer, more circutuatous route to your destination.

I'm a big believer in maintain access. This is why I'm against the push over the decades made by all the ski resorts to expand their boundaries and create link-ups between them. One initiative in particular is to link Alta and Brighton, and Brighton with Park City. It's just a bad idea on so many levels, the most important of which is that it absolutely will take away from the backcountry skiing community terrain that is currently free to access (if you don't count parking.) The terrain I'm talking about is considered by some to be for novices and beginners, but it is also a jumping-off point for more serious terrain for the arrogant elites in the world of backcountry skiing or ski mountaineering. In any case, ski resorts don't need more terrain. More terrain needs to be free, and ski resorts prices are a joke. Increasingly, skiing in-bounds is becoming an activity only for the rich and well-off who can afford to wipe their butts with 100 dollar bills.

There's a tour I'm thinking of, which will go unnamed, that requires me to skin, very briefly, over some private property. I haven't run into any problems doing so, but it is so unfortunate to me that access is technically and lawfully cut off. Going around it, in this particular case, is nearly impossible, and just doesn't make sense no matter how you look at it. So what do you do? Just stop doing such an incredible tour? Or maybe just hope for leniency if you ever got caught? I don't know.

In some places, like the head of Mueller Park Canyon (Mill Canyon on some maps), there is a right-of-way along the GWT or Great Western Trail, but you're not aloud to leave it. Needless to say, if you're restricted to a trail, you're not going to be able to do any worth-while skiing.

Whatever we can do to maintain access and keep the mountains free, in my opinion, is a worthy presuit. The forest service seems to be willing, more and more, to sell its land to private developers. The whole east-site of Francis Peak and Thurstand Peak, for example, has been bought up for the development of a private ski resort! Only the super rich living next to it will be able to use it. It's one thing to make a ski resort, but another to make it so exclusive, especially in a day when ski resorts are so over-crowded. It's just wrong.

Acces is a big issue. I'm not going to take any of the free terrain I enjoy today for granted. One day, who knows. It may be bought up and developed, or all ways of getting to that terrain may get blocked by private property. Just enjoy it all while we can, I suppose.
